A birth chart is a map of the sky from where you were standing at the moment you were born. That is the whole of it. Everything else, every technique and tradition built on top, is an attempt to read that map well.

The most common thing people expect from a chart is a forecast. What they get, when it is read properly, is a description. Those are not the same thing, and the difference is worth understanding before you sit down for a reading.

## The three placements everyone starts with

Most people know their sun sign. It is the one the magazines print, and it describes the core of how you express yourself: what you are lit up by, what you are recognised for.

Your moon sign describes the inner weather. It governs what you need in order to feel safe, how you process feeling, and what you reach for when a day has gone badly. People are often surprised by how much more accurate their moon feels than their sun.

Your rising sign, or ascendant, is the sign that was coming over the horizon at your birth. It is the door people walk through to reach you: first impressions, the manner you meet a room with. It also sets the houses, which is why the birth time matters so much.

## Why the birth time is not a formality

Without a birth time, the rising sign is unknown, and without a rising sign the houses cannot be placed. The houses are the twelve areas of life the planets get distributed across: work, home, partnership, money, the body, the things you keep private.

The planets are what the energy is. The houses are where it lands. A chart with no houses tells you a person is ambitious. A chart with houses tells you whether that ambition is aimed at their career, their family, or their own inner life. One of those is worth an hour of your time. The other is a horoscope.

If you can find your birth time, find it. It is usually on the birth certificate, or a parent remembers within the half hour, which is close enough to work with.

## What a chart describes, and what it does not

A chart describes conditions and tendencies. It shows what you are made of, and what season you are currently in. It is closer to a weather forecast than to a verdict: it tells you to expect rain, it does not tell you whether you take the umbrella.

That distinction is the whole ethic of the practice. Anyone who tells you the chart says you will marry in November, or that a transit means you must not sign the contract, has confused a description with an instruction. Be careful with them.

What a chart is genuinely good for is language. Most people arrive at a reading with a feeling they cannot name and a decision they cannot make. The chart gives them the words. Once you can name the pattern, you can decide what to do about it, which was always your job and never the sky.
